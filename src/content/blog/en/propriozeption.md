You're about to serve. The ball is in your hand, your arm is drawn back — and somehow, without looking, you know whether you're standing too far to the left, whether your arm is too low, whether the distance to the ball is right. You just know. That's proprioception.

## What Is Proprioception?

Proprioception is the sense that tells us the position and movement of our own body — without needing to look. Receptors in muscles, tendons and joints continuously send signals to the brain: How is the arm bent? Where is the centre of gravity? How much tension is in the wrist? All of this happens automatically and mostly unconsciously — but this sense can be trained and deliberately put to use.

## Why Does It Matter So Much in Volleyball?

Volleyball demands precision in almost every situation — and proprioception plays a critical role in nearly all of them:

- **Setting:** Both hands need to contact the ball at exactly the same moment, with minimal contact time.
- **Reception and defence:** The platform has to be precisely aimed at the target while continuously adjusting to the ball's trajectory.
- **Float serve:** The platform needs to stay stable through the moment of contact.
- **Attack:** You have to sense whether the ball will still clear the net, how far the jump has carried you, and whether the line is still within reach.

In all of these situations, there is no time to consciously think it through. The sense has to be there already.

## Can You Train It?

Yes — and faster than you might expect. Athletes who have done sports that systematically develop body awareness — gymnastics, artistic gymnastics, climbing — probably already have a solid foundation. But even without that background, proprioception can be developed quickly. The key is to start listening. Your body is talking all the time; you just need to tune in.

### Amplifying the Signal

If a body signal is too faint to register consciously, external cues help: a piece of tape, a wristband, or simply a small dot on the skin at the spot you want to focus on. The tactile input draws attention there without requiring deliberate effort.

## The Problem: Attention Is a Limited Resource

If it sounds straightforward, why hasn't everyone already developed this sense? The answer is attention bandwidth. We can only focus on a few things at once. When you're occupied with not sending the ball into the net, there's simply no capacity left to also tune into the body.

The same is true of gaze technique — something we covered in the [last article](/blog/en/motorisches-lernen). Few players develop good gaze habits on their own, because the immediate problem consumes all available attention. Poor gaze means less information — which makes every situation harder. Gaze doesn't fix itself; it requires a deliberate focus. Proprioception is no different: it doesn't improve as a side effect.

## Observe — But Don't Interfere

This is the real difficulty, and it's a subtle one. The moment you start consciously monitoring your own mechanics, the internal controller switches on — the part of the brain that tries to micromanage individual muscles. As we saw in the [last article](/blog/en/motorisches-lernen), that's exactly what sabotages you: the body can no longer self-organise, and movement becomes stiffer and less accurate.

So the goal is not to use proprioception to *intervene* — but to *observe*. You keep working with an external focus (a specific contact point on the ball, for example), but at the same time you quietly watch what the body is doing. No correcting, no steering — just noticing.

That sounds easy. It is one of the hardest distinctions to make in training. But once it clicks, it becomes surprisingly natural.

## Actively Freeing Degrees of Freedom

In the [last article](/blog/en/motorisches-lernen) we saw that technique improvements happen through releasing degrees of freedom: joints that were frozen or coupled to other body segments gradually become independent. Proprioception is the tool that lets you actively drive that process — rather than waiting for it to happen on its own.

### A Step-by-Step Example: Setting

Imagine you get feedback that your thumbs are pointing forward during sets. You know it technically — but you don't *feel* it yet. So for a while, you direct your attention to your thumbs: where are they right now? What do they do at contact? Once you can feel them, you switch to an external focus — you want to wrap your hands *around* the ball. You keep observing. And at some point, you notice your wrists are engaging. That solution is more efficient for the body — and it will stick.

### Common Couplings to Look For

- **Attack:** The hip and shoulder are often locked together as a single unit, which prevents good pre-tension. Once you can feel that coupling, you can release it — and your attack improves quickly.
- **Reception and defence:** The shoulder axis is frequently coupled to the hips. For a clean platform alignment in all situations, that coupling needs to go. The approach: focus on the shoulder axis or hips, observe — the release comes by itself.

## Errors Are Part of the Process — When Should You Work on This?

When you start deliberately releasing degrees of freedom, you will initially make more mistakes. That's not a problem — it's the process. A good time to work on this is during the warm-up at the start of training: both sides are still cold and imprecise, errant balls are going to happen anyway. That's exactly when it pays to set an internal focus and observe — with no pressure attached.

Not all focus points are equally easy to hold. Attending to both shoulder and hip simultaneously while still maintaining an external focus takes practice. Again, tape or other tactile aids can help anchor attention at the right place.

## Where to Start

Can we all become exceptional athletes this way? No. But we don't have to stay stuck at our current level — progress is always possible if we're willing to pursue it actively.

The easiest entry point: the resistance band warm-up. Instead of running through the exercises mindlessly while chatting, do them correctly and listen inward at the same time. Within a few weeks, it becomes habit — and then both are possible at once: tuning into the body, doing the exercises right, and still having a conversation.
